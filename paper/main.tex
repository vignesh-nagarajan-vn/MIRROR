\textbf{Abstract.}
Most medical-imaging models stop at a prediction, offering neither the location of
the evidence nor an account a clinician can read. We present \textbf{MIRROR}, a
three-layer, multi-modality radiology pipeline that composes (i) a multi-label
disease classifier, (ii) class-activation-based evidence localization, and (iii) an
evidence-grounded natural-language report generator into a single traceable
system. The pipeline is modality-agnostic: a single registry supplies the finding
taxonomy, anatomical vocabulary, and report phrasing for chest X-ray (14 findings),
brain MRI (11), and head CT (11), and a study is routed to the right classifier
head either explicitly or from its DICOM modality tag. The defining design choice
is that each layer's output is the grounded input to the next, and the language
layer never sees pixels, so every sentence in the generated report traces back to a
predicted probability and a saliency region, and the reasoning layer cannot invent
findings the classifier did not produce. We pair the system with an evaluation
framework that measures predictive quality --- discrimination (AUROC/AUPRC/F1),
clinical operating-point metrics (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV), and
calibration (Brier, ECE) --- and explanation quality (pointing game, localization
IoU) side by side, and an ablation that isolates the cost of the added
interpretability layers. Because those layers are strictly post-hoc, predictive
metrics are provably invariant to them, which lets us frame the contribution as
\emph{interpretability at no predictive cost}. All headline numbers carry bootstrap
$95\%$ confidence intervals and can be aggregated across training seeds, and every
run stamps a reproducibility record.

\section{Introduction}

Deep networks now match specialists on narrow chest-radiograph reading
tasks~\cite{rajpurkar2017chexnet}, yet adoption in the clinic remains limited by
a trust gap: a bare probability tells a radiologist neither \emph{where} the
model is looking nor \emph{why} it reached its conclusion. High-stakes decisions
demand models that can be interrogated, not merely queried~\cite{rudin2019stop}.
Post-hoc saliency methods such as Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} and
Score-CAM~\cite{wang2020scorecam} expose \emph{where} a prediction comes from,
and large language models can render structured evidence as prose, but these
capabilities are rarely composed into one system whose outputs remain mutually
consistent and auditable end to end.

\textbf{MIRROR} integrates all three into a single pipeline:
\[
\text{Image}\rightarrow\text{Prediction}\rightarrow\text{Localization}
\rightarrow\text{Reasoning}\rightarrow\text{Report}.
\]
Its central design constraint is \emph{grounding}: the language layer is given
only the structured evidence produced upstream (labels, probabilities, and named
saliency regions), never the raw image. This makes hallucinated findings
structurally impossible, since the report can only discuss what the classifier
predicted and the localizer situated, and it makes the whole chain traceable. The
same pipeline runs unchanged across three imaging modalities --- chest X-ray, brain
MRI, and head CT --- because only the finding taxonomy, the anatomical vocabulary,
and a little report phrasing differ between them; these are centralized in one
modality registry, and a study is routed to the correct classifier head either by
an explicit selection or from its DICOM modality tag. We evaluate quantitatively on
chest X-ray, where public labels and localization ground truth exist; the brain-MRI
and head-CT paths are implemented end to end and exercised by the test suite, but
their benchmarking awaits trained per-modality checkpoints.

\paragraph{Research question.} Can a multimodal system that combines image
classification, visual explainability, and language generation improve
interpretability without degrading predictive performance, relative to a
classification-only baseline? We build the system so this question is
\emph{measurable}: predictive and explanation metrics live side by side, and an
ablation reproduces the classification-only baseline the question names.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A three-layer, evidence-grounded radiology pipeline in which the language
  layer reasons only over structured upstream evidence, so every report sentence
  is traceable to a probability and a saliency region.
  \item A grounding constraint that makes the generated report auditable and
  non-hallucinatory \emph{by construction}, together with a post-hoc layer design
  whose invariance to the predictions is verified empirically.
  \item A modality-agnostic realization of that pipeline across three imaging
  modalities (chest X-ray, brain MRI, head CT), driven by a single registry that is
  the one source of truth for each modality's label taxonomy, anatomical
  vocabulary, and report phrasing, with routing either explicit or auto-detected
  from the DICOM modality tag, so adding a modality is a data change rather than a
  code change.
  \item An open-source implementation spanning interchangeable backbones
  (DenseNet-121, EfficientNet-B0, ViT-B/16), two explainability methods
  (Grad-CAM, Score-CAM), an LLM report backend with a deterministic offline
  fallback, native DICOM ingest, and a web front end served by two interchangeable
  inference engines behind one response contract (Fig.~\ref{fig:ui}).
  \item An evaluation framework that scores prediction and explanation quality
  jointly, with bootstrap confidence intervals, multi-seed aggregation, and an
  ablation isolating the predictive cost of interpretability, so the research
  question can be answered quantitatively. \pending{fold in the measured result
  once the model is trained.}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}

\paragraph{Datasets and classification.} Large, labeled chest-radiograph corpora
made supervised deep learning on this modality practical. The NIH ChestX-ray8/14
release~\cite{wang2017chestxray} provides 112{,}120 frontal radiographs over 14
pathologies and remains a standard benchmark; CheXpert~\cite{irvin2019chexpert}
and MIMIC-CXR~\cite{johnson2019mimiccxr} extended scale, uncertainty labeling,
and paired free-text reports. On these benchmarks CheXNet~\cite{rajpurkar2017chexnet}
showed a 121-layer DenseNet~\cite{huang2017densenet} reaching radiologist-level
pneumonia detection. Convolutional backbones such as
EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} and, more recently, Vision
Transformers~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} now form the standard toolkit; MIRROR
keeps all three interchangeable and adopts DenseNet-121 as its primary
configuration for comparability.

\paragraph{Visual explanation.} Class Activation Mapping~\cite{zhou2016cam}
localized the evidence behind a CNN prediction; Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam}
generalized it to arbitrary architectures via gradients, and
Score-CAM~\cite{wang2020scorecam} removed the gradient dependence with a
perturbation-based weighting. Model-agnostic attribution methods, notably
LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016lime} and SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017shap}, explain individual
predictions without access to model internals. To move from qualitative heatmaps
to measurable evidence, the pointing-game protocol~\cite{zhang2016pointing}
and intersection-over-union against annotated lesions provide quantitative
localization scores, which MIRROR reports directly.

\paragraph{Rigor and critique of saliency.} Explanations are only useful if they
are faithful. Adebayo et al.~\cite{adebayo2018sanity} showed several saliency
methods can be insensitive to model and data, and argued for sanity checks; Rudin
~\cite{rudin2019stop} cautioned against post-hoc explanation of black boxes for
high-stakes decisions; and Doshi-Velez and Kim~\cite{doshivelez2017rigorous}
called for a rigorous, measurable science of interpretability. These critiques
motivate two of MIRROR's design choices: quantitative localization evaluation
against ground-truth boxes, and hard grounding so the language layer cannot
outrun the evidence.

\paragraph{Report generation.} Systems that emit radiology prose range from
TieNet~\cite{wang2018tienet}, which jointly embeds image and text, to
memory-driven transformers such as R2Gen~\cite{chen2020r2gen}, to clinically
focused decoders optimized for factual correctness~\cite{liu2019clinically}. A
recurring failure mode is hallucination: prose generated directly from pixels can
assert findings the underlying model never detected. MIRROR sidesteps this by
restricting generation to structured, verified evidence, trading open-ended
fluency for auditability.

\paragraph{Explainable AI in medicine and positioning.} Surveys of explainable AI
for medical imaging~\cite{tjoa2021survey} note that interpretability and report
generation are usually studied in isolation. MIRROR's contribution is not a new
classifier or a new saliency method, but their \emph{grounded composition} into
one traceable pipeline, together with a framework that measures whether adding
the explanation and reasoning layers helps interpretability without a predictive
penalty.

\label{sec:method}

next (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\subsection{Layer 1: Classification}
The classifier is a factory over three ImageNet-pretrained backbones: DenseNet-121
(default), EfficientNet-B0, and ViT-B/16, with a multi-label head whose width $N$ is
the number of findings in the active modality's taxonomy (14 for chest X-ray, 11
each for brain MRI and head CT; sigmoid applied at loss and inference time, so the
network emits raw logits). Inputs are resized to $224\times224$ and normalized with
ImageNet statistics. Training uses \code{BCEWithLogitsLoss}, AdamW (learning rate
$10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$), a cosine schedule, and dropout $0.2$;
checkpoints are selected on best validation macro-AUROC. For chest X-ray the 14
labels follow the canonical NIH ChestX-ray14 taxonomy, kept as a single source of
truth shared by the classifier head, the report generator, and the evaluation
scripts. Because real radiology data arrives as DICOM rather than PNG/JPEG, a
dedicated ingest applies the modality LUT (rescale slope/intercept), the VOI
(window) LUT, and MONOCHROME1 inversion before handing the pipeline a display-ready
image, lifts only non-PHI technical tags (modality, view position, laterality), and
exposes the DICOM modality tag for automatic routing.

\subsection{Layer 2: Evidence Localization}
For each positive label (top-$k$, $k{=}3$ by default, to bound compute), the
explainability layer computes a class-activation map, either Grad-CAM
(forward/backward hooks on a resolved target layer) or Score-CAM (gradient-free,
perturbation-based), and reduces it to a named region so the evidence can be
referred to in words. Target layers are resolved per backbone; ViT maps are
recovered by reshaping the patch-token sequence back to a $14\times14$ spatial
grid. The activated region's centroid is mapped into a $3\times3$ anatomical grid
in radiology convention (patient-right on the viewer's left), using the modality's
plane to pick the vocabulary --- lung zones for a frontal chest film (``the right
lower zone'') or lobar regions for an axial brain-MRI/head-CT slice (``the left
frontal region'') --- and a colormap overlay is rendered for the UI
(Fig.~\ref{fig:qual}, left).

\subsection{Layer 3: Clinical Reasoning}
The reasoning layer converts structured evidence (labels, probabilities, and
named regions) into a \textsc{Findings}/\textsc{Impression} report via a system
prompt plus an evidence-grounded user prompt. Per-label plain-English
descriptions ground the model so it need not recall what each token means. The
backend is an LLM (Claude) with a deterministic offline template fallback so the
system always yields a coherent report even with no network or API key; generation
runs at low temperature for stability, and the LLM backend falls back to the
template on any error. Predictions below the confidence threshold ($0.5$) are
surfaced as pertinent negatives rather than silently dropped. Because both
backends consume identical evidence, swapping them changes \emph{how fluently} the
report reads, never \emph{what} it may say; and because the layer never receives
the image, it cannot introduce findings absent from the upstream evidence
(Fig.~\ref{fig:qual}, right).

\subsection{Multi-modality via a modality registry}
The three layers above are modality-agnostic: swapping a chest radiograph for a
brain MRI or a head CT changes only the finding taxonomy the head predicts, the
anatomical vocabulary a saliency centroid is named in (lung zones vs.\ lobar
regions), and a little report phrasing (so a normal brain study reads ``no acute
intracranial abnormality,'' not ``no acute cardiopulmonary abnormality''). These
per-modality details are centralized in one registry (Table~\ref{tab:modalities}),
which is the single source of truth for each modality's labels, label glosses,
imaging plane, and report guidance; a hand-kept TypeScript mirror lets the hosted
engine and UI selector share the identical taxonomy and \emph{ordering} (output
index $i$ maps to $\text{labels}[i]$). A study's modality is resolved either from an
explicit selection or, for a DICOM input, from its (0008,0060) modality tag
(\code{MR}$\rightarrow$brain MRI, \code{CT}$\rightarrow$head CT,
\code{CR}/\code{DX}$\rightarrow$chest). The orchestrator builds and caches one
classifier--explainer engine per modality on demand, so a single deployment serves
all three. Adding a fourth modality is therefore a data change --- one registry
entry and (once trained) a checkpoint --- rather than a change to the pipeline.

\subsection{Orchestration and Serving}
A single \code{analyze()} entry point runs the stages and returns one result
object (predictions, overlays, report) consumed by both the REST API and the
notebooks; the two later layers are individually toggleable, which recovers the
ablation conditions of Section~\ref{sec:setup} and records per-stage wall-clock
timings. Two interchangeable serving engines satisfy the \emph{same} JSON response
contract (Table~\ref{tab:topology}), so the web UI (Fig.~\ref{fig:ui}) is identical
regardless of which engine answered: a finding carries \emph{either} a rendered
Grad-CAM overlay (local) \emph{or} a normalized bounding box (hosted), and the same
viewer draws whichever is present. The hosted engine exists because the PyTorch
pipeline cannot fit typical serverless limits (no GPU, a small bundle ceiling,
cold-start model loads); it substitutes a vision LLM constrained to a structured
tool schema that emits the identical JSON, and with no API key returns a
clearly-labelled deterministic demo result so the deployed site never hard-fails.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

The evaluation is designed to answer the research question's two halves,
prediction and explanation, with parallel harnesses that emit machine-readable
summaries the tables below are generated from. \emph{The design is fixed; the
numbers await a trained checkpoint.}

\paragraph{Data.} We use NIH ChestX-ray14~\cite{wang2017chestxray}: 112{,}120
frontal radiographs from 30{,}805 patients, labeled across 14 pathologies
(multi-label), with the official train/validation and held-out test splits.
Quantitative evaluation is confined to this chest-X-ray modality, where public
multi-label annotations and lesion boxes exist; the evaluation harness is
modality-parametric (a \code{-{}-modality} flag selects the taxonomy and output
filename), so the brain-MRI and head-CT paths regenerate the same metric panel once
labeled test data and trained per-modality checkpoints are available.

\paragraph{Localization ground truth.} We score heatmaps against the official
\code{BBox\_List\_2017.csv}: $\sim$984 hand-drawn boxes over 8 of the 14
pathologies (Atelectasis, Cardiomegaly, Effusion, Infiltration, Mass, Nodule,
Pneumonia, Pneumothorax). Boxes are given in original $1024\times1024$ pixel
coordinates and rescaled into the model's heatmap grid; the remaining six labels
have no box ground truth and are not scored.

\paragraph{Predictive metrics.} We report a clinical-reader panel on the official
test split, in three families. \emph{Discrimination:} per-label and macro AUROC,
per-label and macro AUPRC (more informative than AUROC under the heavy class
imbalance of medical findings), and macro F1 at a $0.5$ threshold.
\emph{Operating point:} at the decision threshold, the quantities a clinician reads
off a model --- sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and NPV --- per label with positive
support counts and macro-averaged, so a headline AUROC is accompanied by the
false-negative/false-positive trade-off it implies. \emph{Calibration:} the Brier
score and Expected Calibration Error (10 equal-width bins), because a probability a
clinician is asked to trust must mean what it says. Each headline metric carries a
bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval: the test set is resampled with replacement
$B{=}1000$ times, and a \emph{single} resample drives all metrics at once (AUROC,
F1, sensitivity, specificity, Brier), so the intervals are mutually consistent. For
a statistic $\theta$ with bootstrap replicates $\{\hat\theta^{*}_b\}$ we report the
two-sided percentile interval
$[\hat\theta^{*}_{(\alpha/2)},\,\hat\theta^{*}_{(1-\alpha/2)}]$ with
$\alpha{=}0.05$, which supports statements of the form ``config A beats config B.''

\paragraph{Explanation metrics.} Following~\cite{zhang2016pointing} and the
ChestX-ray8 localization protocol~\cite{wang2017chestxray}: (i) \emph{pointing
game}, does the heatmap's peak fall inside the lesion box; (ii) \emph{mean IoU},
overlap between the box and the heatmap thresholded at $0.5$; and (iii)
\emph{localization accuracy}, the fraction of boxes with IoU $\ge 0.1$ (the
T(IoU) measure).

\paragraph{Ablation.} Three conditions isolate the effect of the added layers
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation}): \code{classification\_only}, \code{with\_localization},
and \code{full\_mirror}. Because layers 2--3 are post-hoc, the predictive
metrics are expected to be identical across conditions; the harness verifies this
empirically and reports the maximum prediction delta, so any change in
interpretability can be attributed cleanly to added capability rather than a
different model.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Runs are seeded (default $42$); robustness to
training stochasticity is summarized across seeds $\{0,1,2\}$ as mean~$\pm$~std,
reported alongside the per-model bootstrap CIs. Every results file stamps a
reproducibility record (seed, git commit, library versions).

over the bundled synthetic samples for \emph{all three} modalities
(chest X-ray, brain MRI, head CT; $12$ studies each) yields a maximum
probability delta of exactly $0$ in every case --- the localization and report
layers leave the predictions bitwise unchanged. The live per-stage profile on the

\section{Discussion}

\pending{Interpret the tables and graphs once real numbers are available.
Anticipated storyline: (1) the classifier reaches the CheXNet-range AUROC expected
on ChestX-ray14, and its operating point (sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV) and
calibration (Brier/ECE) are read alongside it; (2) the localization results
substantiate the novelty claim that the heatmaps land on annotated lesions; (3) the
ablation shows the added layers leave every predictive metric unchanged; (4)
contrast Grad-CAM vs.\ Score-CAM localization; (5) once per-modality checkpoints
exist, report the same panel for brain MRI and head CT, testing whether the
grounded-composition recipe transfers across modalities.}

Independent of the trained numbers, MIRROR's grounding constraint is a
qualitative contribution. Because the language layer sees only structured
evidence, the generated report is auditable by construction and cannot assert
findings the classifier did not produce; this trades open-ended fluency for a form
of safety that matters in a clinical setting. The post-hoc placement of layers
2--3 is likewise not merely convenient: because the classifier's logits are
untouched, any change in a predictive metric across ablation conditions would
signal a bug, and the harness checks for exactly that, making the central claim
falsifiable. Finally, serving one JSON contract from both a heavyweight local
PyTorch stack and a lightweight hosted vision-LLM engine shows that the
\emph{interface} MIRROR tests (predict $\rightarrow$ show evidence $\rightarrow$
explain) is independent of the particular inference backend.

\section{Ethics, Safety, and Limitations}

\textbf{Not a medical device.} MIRROR is a research prototype, not reviewed or
cleared by any regulatory body; every output is a draft that must be verified by a
licensed radiologist and must not be used for diagnosis or treatment. Each report
carries that disclaimer explicitly.

\textbf{Grounded by design.} The language layer never sees pixels, only structured
evidence, so it cannot invent findings; below-threshold predictions are reported
as pertinent negatives rather than dropped, and predictions are surfaced as
probabilities to keep uncertainty explicit.

\textbf{Data governance.} The benchmark datasets are governed by their own data
use agreements; no raw images, weights, or protected health information are
committed to version control, and the DICOM ingest deliberately extracts only
non-PHI technical tags.

\textbf{Limitations.} The reported numbers are format-illustrative placeholders,
not measured results; real training and evaluation on ChestX-ray14 are required
before any quantitative claim. Multi-modality is a structural, not yet an empirical,
result: the brain-MRI and head-CT paths are implemented end to end --- registry,
per-modality classifier head, DICOM auto-routing, modality-aware localization
vocabulary and report phrasing, all exercised by the torch-free test suite --- but
they are not yet quantitatively benchmarked, because that needs trained per-modality
checkpoints and labeled test sets we do not redistribute. Quantitative evaluation is
therefore currently confined to chest radiographs, and localization ground truth
exists for only 8 of 14 chest pathologies. Report quality is presently assessed
qualitatively; a quantitative comparison against real radiologist reports (for
example MIMIC-CXR~\cite{johnson2019mimiccxr}) is future work. Class-activation maps
are coarse and can highlight context rather than the lesion
itself~\cite{adebayo2018sanity}, and the $3\times3$ location grid is a deliberately
coarse anatomical descriptor.

\section{Conclusion}

MIRROR composes classification, evidence localization, and grounded report
generation into a single traceable, multi-modality radiology pipeline --- one
registry-driven system spanning chest X-ray, brain MRI, and head CT --- paired with
an evaluation framework that measures prediction and explanation quality side by
side and, via an ablation that holds predictions invariant, frames the
contribution as interpretability added at no predictive cost. \pending{state the
measured outcome and the ablation result once the model is trained.} The immediate
next step is to replace the illustrative results with real numbers from a trained
checkpoint, then benchmark the same evidence-grounded recipe on the head-CT and
brain-MRI modalities already implemented end to end in the codebase. The system and
its evaluation harnesses are released open-source to support reproducible study of
interpretability and trust in medical imaging.
